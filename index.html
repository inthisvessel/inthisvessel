How I'm wired

Most teams can tell you what users do. I'm trained to explain why — and design around it.

01

Psychology

A degree in human behavior, not just interfaces. I design for how people actually decide under stress, doubt, and distraction — accounting for trust, cognitive load, motivation, and the mental models they bring before they ever touch a screen.

02

Research

Evidence over opinion. I go where the system meets real people — clinics, call floors, live production booths — and map exactly where intention breaks down into friction, so decisions rest on what people do, not what they say they'll do.

03

Product Design

Insight only counts if it ships. I turn behavioral findings into flows, systems, and defensible tradeoffs that move real metrics — and that keep working after I've handed them off.

Psychology explains the behavior. Research locates it. Product design resolves it — that loop is what I bring to a team.

How I think

Every decision earns its place — from evidence, not taste.

The same loop runs through every project. Here it is on Closing the Care Gap, where it cut specialist referral times from over a year to about two weeks.

01 · Research

Go to the friction

I observe the system where it meets real people — not where it's documented.

Shadowed the clinic for six weeks and audited a full year of referrals.

02 · Insight

Name the real problem

I interpret findings into a "so what" — the cause, not the symptom.

The delay wasn't clinical. It was an invisible handoff with no owner.

03 · Decision

Make a defensible call

I weigh options against constraints and commit to a tradeoff I can defend.

Chose one shared referral tracker over a new patient-facing app — feasible for the team to sustain.

04 · Outcome

Ship something that holds

I measure the change and design it to survive without me.

Referral processing fell from 12+ months to ~2 weeks — and held after I left.

Research → insight → decision → outcome. The decision log in each case study traces this loop end to end — so you can audit the reasoning, not just admire the result.

I'm a psychology-trained product designer in my final year at Kennesaw State University. For four years — across healthcare, banking, and operations — I've worked inside the systems where people and process collide, and studied the behavioral science that explains why they break.

My psychology background isn't adjacent to design — it's the foundation of how I design. I read for the things interfaces usually miss: what earns trust, what quietly overloads people, and what makes them give up one step before the finish.

So I don't start with screens. I start with the behavior — then design the system that moves the outcome.
